An intelligent creator is the best explanation for our existence.

There is no scientifically sound theory for an unguided, materialistic origin of life.

The Prebiotic Soup

Some scientists have suggested that life could have arisen on Earth from a "prebiotic soup", a theoretical pool of all the chemicals that make up a living cell. However, there is no positive evidence that such a "soup" ever existed [8].

If the prebiotic soup had existed it would have had to contain a large amount of nitrogen because amino acids are nitrogenous. However, the earliest sediments of Earth are not nitrogen rich as would be expected if a prebiotic soup had existed in any large quantity. Sediments with large deposits of nitrogen have never been found [8].

Further, even if the prebiotic soup had existed, it would have caused any amino acids in it to quickly react with the other chemicals present and form substances not conducive to the creation of life [8].

The Miller-Urey Experiment

In 1952, Stanley Miller performed an experiment in which he formed amino acids by shooting electricity through a mixture of gases that his doctoral advisor, Harold Urey, thought was similar to the atmosphere of primitive Earth[2]. Proteins, which are vital to the function of a living cell, are made from specific types of amino acids. This experiment is thought to demonstrate how life could have been spontaneously created from non-living matter.

However, there are a couple of aspects of the Miller-Urey experiment that make it an unlikely explanation for the origin of life.

First, it is now thought that Earth's early atmosphere probably contained much less hydrogen than Miller and Urey assumed. The same experiment performed on a more realistic atmosphere results in chemicals such as formaldehyde and cyanide, substances unlikely to form biological material in an unguided process [2].

Second, even if the experiment were a credible success, going from amino acid to protein to living cell is absurdly unlikely in an undirected process [2]. No scientist has ever been able to create a living cell, even in a carefully contrived and directed environment containing all of the necessary substances and none of the harmful ones. So, how likely is it that a living cell would form on its own by chance in less-than-ideal conditions with no intelligent intervention?

Random Chance Plus Time

Another oft-heard proposal for how life could have emerged naturally is that it just happened by random chance. In other words, even though the probability is very small, it can still happen over an extremely large period of time.

The truth is that the odds of life emerging by chance are so incomprehensibly small that no scientist proposes this as a sufficient explanation for the origin of life anymore. Microfossils have been used to estimate that the time between the Earth cooling to the point it could support life and the time life first appeared was only about 400 million years. Even under absurdly optimized conditions this is nowhere near enough time for even a single protein molecule to form by chance, much less a complete single-celled organism [13].

Why is it so unlikely for a protein molecule to form by chance? A protein molecule has a minimum number of amino acids it must have in order for it to function in an organism, all of these amino acids must be "left-handed" (the only one of the two possible varieties of amino acids found in living beings), and they all must be assembled in the correct order. All of this together makes the probability of forming even a single protein molecule by chance prohibitively small, let alone the at least 300 proteins required by a simple living cell [8].

Self-Organizational Properties

It has also been suggested that self-ordering tendencies in the form of bonding affinities could explain the natural formation of life, similar to the properties of salt that cause it to form crystals when a saturated salt solution evaporates.

However, amino acids don't have any significant bonding affinities amongst themselves, and the slight ones they do have don't correspond to any of the known sequence patterns in working proteins. So, the formation of amino acids into proteins can't be explained by self-organization [8].

Additionally, the four chemical "letters" (the bases adenine, guanine, cytosine, and thymine), whose sequence in DNA tells a cell how to construct amino acids, don't have any bonding or attraction among themselves that causes them to be arranged in the necessary order. They only bond with the sugar and phosphate molecules that make up the two "backbones" of the DNA chain, and each base is equally capable of bonding to any point in the chain [8].

Even if there were bonding affinities among amino acids or the DNA bases, they would be unlikely to adequately explain the vast amount of information found in living beings. Naturally occurring self-ordering tendencies tend to result in short and repetitive patterns rather than complex and varied sequences like those found in DNA [13].

There are significant reasons to doubt that all life on Earth is descended from a common ancestor via evolution.

The Fossil Record.

The fossil record presents a challenge to the theory of gradual descent from a common ancestor due to a period known as the Cambrian explosion that scientists believe occurred about 540 million years ago. The Cambrian explosion is so named because the fossils from this period show most of the major animal phyla alive today appearing over a period of time that is very abrupt compared with the rest of Earth's history[2]. Contrary to the tree model predicted by gradual descent, the Cambrian explosion exhibits a "top down" pattern in that all the "major differences in form and body plans appear first" without any transitional forms, and then smaller variations on these forms appear later[8].

One proposed explanation for this sudden appearance of phyla in the fossil record is that the bodies of pre-Cambrian organisms were too soft to form fossils. However, this is an unlikely explanation given that fossils of soft-bodied creatures have been found both from before and during the Cambrian explosion[2].

From the biological information perspective, the Cambrian explosion is an issue in that it requires an explanation for how all of the new DNA information involved suddenly appeared rather than gradually arising through mutation and natural selection. Some scientists believe that DNA mutation and natural selection are insufficient to explain the appearance of "new biological forms" because DNA does not contain all of the information needed for creating different kinds of cells, tissues, and organs and placing them in the right spot at the right time during embryonic development. This means a tremendous amount of new information would have to have suddenly appeared in all the right places for the Cambrian explosion to occur[8].

Irreducible Complexity

The concept of "irreducible complexity" in biological systems is one of the more significant challenges to the idea of gradual descent from a common ancestor. An irreducibly complex system consists of multiple components that interact to perform a specific task, with each component being vital to the successful completion of that task.

According to Charles Darwin, "if it could be demonstrated that any complex organ existed which could not possibly have been formed by numerous, successive, slight modifications, my theory would absolutely break down.[34]" While it is perhaps unlikely that anyone will ever be able to prove that they "could not possibly have been formed" by evolution, irreducibly complex systems in organisms do exist, and so far scientists have been unable to come up with satisfactory evolutionary explanations for how they were assembled[7]. Some examples of irreducibly complex biological systems include cilia, bacterial flagella, and the blood clotting process.

Cilia, hairs on the outside of some cells used for propulsion or for movement of outside substances, are composed of microtubules, nexin linkers, and motor proteins (dynein). The complete removal of any of these causes cilia to fail[7].

Flagella are propeller-like systems used by many bacteria cells for movement. A functional flagellum requires 30 to 35 proteins that make up at least three parts: a paddle, rotor, and motor. The absence of any one of these parts completely disables the flagellum[7].

Blood clotting is accomplished via "a highly choreographed cascade of ten steps that use about 20 different molecular components," a delicate process that, if not done in the right place, time, and quantity, can be fatal (e.g. clotting in the brain). Without the whole system in place, an animal would either bleed to death or die from clots forming in inappropriate places. It is unlikely that blood clotting could have formed via a gradual process using natural selection because an animal with a partially-completed blood clotting system would either bleed to death or die of improperly regulated clotting, thereby actually eliminating that animal naturally rather than selecting it [7].

There are logically sound arguments and significant scientific support for an intelligent creator of the universe and of life.

Information in DNA

DNA contains the genetic information necessary for creating all of the proteins used by the organism to which it belongs, and this information is accessed and utilized by a specific cellular process. To explain the origin of life, one must explain the origin of this information[8].

"Information" in DNA refers to the specific linear sequences of four chemicals commonly referred to as "bases": adenine, guanine, cytosine, and thymine. These sequences provide the cell with the specific patterns needed to construct all of the different types of proteins used by the organism. This is comparable to the way specific sequences of letters in the alphabet convey information by forming different words[8].

There is a tremendous amount of this information stored in DNA. Just one type of protein is built using a specific sequence of anywhere from 1200 to 2000 of the DNA bases [8], and the DNA found in a single human cell contains more information than the entirety of the Encyclopaedia Britannica[13].

When we find sequential information that corresponds with a "functional requirement" (an independent specification for interpreting the meaning of the sequence) in any other context, we automatically and reasonably assume that it came from an intelligent source; why not do the same with DNA?[8]

"Information is not something derived from material properties; in a sense, it transcends matter and energy. Naturalistic theories that rely solely on matter and energy are not going to be able to account for information. Only intelligence can…. we know [information we interact with daily] invariably comes from intelligent agents. So what do we make of the fact that there's information in life? What do we make of the fact that DNA stores far more information in a smaller space than the most advanced supercomputer on the planet?"[8]

The Big Bang

The Big Bang theory supports the idea of a creator in that it states that our universe (energy, matter, space, and time) all had a beginning, implying the need for a cause that exists outside of these domains[3]. While initially met with skepticism and distrust, the Big Bang theory has been consistently validated by supporting scientific predictions and discoveries.

In 1915 when Albert Einstein developed the theory of general relativity, he discovered that it predicted that the universe is either expanding or contracting. In the 1920s Alexander Friedman and George Lemaître developed models that predicted the universe is expanding, leading to the conclusion that going back in time, it had a point of origin—this is when the theory was mockingly christened "The Big Bang" by astronomer Fred Hoyle. These models were supported in 1929 by Edwin Hubble's discovery that distant galaxies appear more red than they ought, indicating that they are moving away from us at very high speeds. In 1940, George Gamow deduced that if the Big Bang model's prediction of a highly dense initial universe were true, then currently the background temperature of the universe should be just slightly above absolute zero, and it was discovered in 1965 that this background temperature is indeed a mere 3.7 degrees above absolute zero. Finally, while we know that heavier elements such as iron are formed inside of stars, the formation of lighter elements such as helium require much, much hotter temperatures than are present even in the center of stars, suggesting that these elements must have been formed in the extremely high temperatures predicted to exist at the start of the Big Bang[4].

Fine-Tuning of the Universe

The fine-tuning of the universe suggests that an intelligent creator was its cause. The "fine-tuning of the universe" refers to "the extraordinary balancing of the fundamental laws and parameters of physics and the initial conditions of the universe," which has enabled our universe to support life. There are a wide variety of these parameters that, if changed only slightly, would result in a universe incapable of sustaining life[5]

One such parameter is the difference in mass between protons and neutrons. If the mass of the neutron were 1/700 larger, nuclear fusion in stars would not be possible, meaning they could not exist. Without stars, there is no source of energy for a life-sustaining planet[5].

Another has to do with the way hydrogen is converted to helium. As part of this process, 0.007 (seven thousandths) of the mass of the hydrogen is converted to energy. If this number were 0.006, then the transformation couldn't take place at all and the universe would consist solely of hydrogen. If it were 0.008, then the universe would have run out of hydrogen long ago[35].

It is also very likely that the initial conditions of the universe were very fine-tuned. Many scientists subscribe to inflation theory, which states that the initial rate of expansion of the universe was extremely high and then slowed later, and which conveniently avoids the highly-specialized initial conditions required by the standard, non-inflationary model of the Big Bang by pushing them "beyond the range of what we can observe". However, there is no empirical evidence that proves inflation has occurred, and none of the numerous models for it have ever been generally accepted[4].

So many other examples of the fine-tuning of the universe exist that it is exceedingly unlikely that all of them came about by chance to form our universe. Some scientists have proposed that our universe is just one of an extremely large number of universes and therefore ours happens to be the universe in which all of this fine-tuning occurred randomly. However, the existing multi-verse theories are highly speculative, and there is as yet no sound empirical evidence that any universe exists beyond our own[5].

The Earth meets a wide variety of requirements for a life-sustaining planet that are exceedingly unlikely to all have come about by pure chance.

Earth's Galaxy

There are several properties of the Milky Way galaxy and Earth's position in it that make it possible for life to exist here, as well as properties of other galaxies that make it impossible for them to produce or support habitable planets.

Despite the large number of stars in a galaxy, habitable planets are only possible in certain places. In fact, of the three types of galaxies (spiral, elliptical, and irregular), only a spiral galaxy has areas that can potentially support such a planet. Elliptical galaxies have erratically orbiting stars which pass through dangerous parts of the galaxy, and they don't contain enough heavy elements to produce life-sustaining planets. Irregular galaxies have no safe spots because supernovae occur at the same rate everywhere within them.[6]

In a spiral galaxy like the Milky Way, only the areas between the arms of the spiral could contain a safe, life-sustaining planet. The center has more frequently occurring supernovae, and at least in the Milky Way, there is a massive black hole that releases a variety of deadly radiation any time matter gets into or near it. The arms themselves are also hazardous due to frequent supernovae, and contain giant molecular clouds in which life could not survive.[6]

In the Milky Way specifically the thinness of the galaxy's disk shape helps the sun stay in a circular orbit, keeping it within the safe zone rather than having it enter the central region of the galaxy or one of the dangerous spiral arms as it would if it followed a more irregular orbit.[6]

Earth's Solar System

There are many specific properties of the sun, the other planets in Earth's solar system, and Earth's position relative to these other members of the system that make it possible for life to survive on Earth.

The Earth is positioned within the sun's Circumstellar Habitable Zone at an ideal distance for supporting life, and remains at that distance due to its near-perfect circular orbit. The Circumstellar Habitable Zone is the area around a star where liquid water can exist on the surface of an Earth-like planet: too close to the star and it all evaporates; too far and it all freezes. The further a planet is from the sun, the more CO2 it needs to trap the sun's radiation and keep water liquid. However, too much CO2 in the atmosphere results in there being insufficient oxygen to support mammal life. So, there is only a small area of the Circumstellar Habitable Zone in which an Earth-like planet can exist, and it would need a circular orbit like Earth's to stay in that area.[6]

The sun possesses several properties uncommon to stars in general that are necessary for life on an orbiting planet to survive. First, the mass of the sun allows Earth to orbit far enough away to keep solar flares from causing drastic, deadly climate changes, but close enough to maintain liquid water. This isn't possible with red dwarf stars, which are smaller than the sun and make up roughly 80% of the stars in the universe. Second, it emits just the right balance of red and blue light, unlike red dwarfs which don't emit enough ultraviolet light to build up oxygen in the atmosphere at a sufficient rate, or F-type stars which are larger than the sun and emit so much ultraviolet light that any small breach in the ozone layer would instantly bombard the unprotected area with deadly amounts of UV radiation. Third, if the sun were more massive, it wouldn't live nearly as long and its luminosity would change at a much higher rate during its main sequence (the time during which a star is undergoing fusion of hydrogen into helium), resulting in more significant temperature variations on Earth over a shorter period of time. Finally, the sun is very stable compared to other similar stars, varying in luminosity by only one tenth of a percent over a sunspot cycle (approximately 11 years). This helps prevent drastic shifts in Earth's climate.[6]

The other planets in Earth's solar system also contribute to its habitability. The majority of the other gas giant planets discovered orbiting stars in other solar systems have very elliptical orbits and would therefore throw off the circularity of the orbit of any Earth-like planet in the same system, causing it to undergo life-threatening temperature variations. By contrast, Jupiter has a near-circular orbit and so does not cause this issue for Earth. Planets in Earth's solar system also protect it from being struck by other celestial bodies. Jupiter acts as a comet shield for Earth, as do Saturn and Uranus to a lesser degree, and Mars and Venus shield Earth from asteroids.[6]

Earth's Moon

Certain properties of Earth's moon contribute to sustaining life on Earth.

First, the moon's size helps regulate the temperatures on Earth. Its large size compared to Earth helps to stabilize the tilt of Earth's axis, preventing Earth from tilting to drastic angles which would result in major differences in seasonal temperatures. However if the moon were larger, it would slow down Earth's rotation, causing greater differences in temperature between night and day.[6]

Second, the moon accounts for 60% of the tides on Earth. The tides are important for pulling nutrients from the continents out into the ocean, and they also contribute to large-scale ocean circulation, which moderates the temperatures closer to the poles.

Finally, scientists now think the moon was probably formed by a glancing collision of Earth and a celestial body the size of Mars. So unless there is a more common way for moons like the Earth's to form, this suggests that planets capable of sustaining life may be as rare as such collisions.[36]

Earth Itself

The Earth itself has several properties that support its habitability.

First, consider the Earth's mass. If it were too large, the Earth would be unable to retain an atmosphere or keep from losing heat too quickly. However, if it were too large, gravity would level the surface to the point where it would be completely submerged in water. Eventually, the water would become too salty to support even ocean life because salt is removed from the ocean by rapid evaporation in shallow coastal areas.[6]

Second, Earth has marine algae that naturally regulate its albedo (proportion of sunlight reflected vs. absorbed), which in turn regulates temperature. These marine algae release dimethyl sulfide which helps build cloud condensation nuclei (particles in the atmosphere upon which water condenses to form clouds). When the ocean gets warmer the algae reproduce at a higher rate, releasing more dimethyl sulfide; increased cloud cover increases albedo and the ocean cools as a result, bringing down the rate of algae reproduction and corresponding release of dimethyl sulfide.[6]

Finally, Earth contains a variety of elements in all the right places necessary for life to survive and thrive. There are several properties of water and carbon that make them vital for life—non-carbon-based life forms could not work because other elements are incapable of forming complex-enough molecules. Further, a bacterium requires approximately 16 different essential elements to survive, and humans require 26. A planet would have to have sufficient quantities of at least the 16 to support even very simple life.[6] In addition to the elements required by organisms themselves, the presence of radioactive elements deep in the Earth supports the convection of liquid iron surrounding the core, creating a dynamo which generates Earth's magnetic field. The magnetic field protects Earth from radiation from low-energy cosmic rays, and it also protects the upper atmosphere from being stripped away by solar winds, which would result in water being lost too quickly.[6] Finally, the availability near the Earth's surface of the ores used in the technological advancement of civilization is a result of a very particular series of circumstances and conditions.[38]
